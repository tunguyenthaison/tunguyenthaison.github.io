On $\T^n$

On $\T^n$, the set $\{E_\kappa(x): \kappa\in \Z^n\}$, where $E_\kappa(x) = e^{2\pi i \kappa \cdot x}$, is an orthonormal basis of the Hilbert space $L^2(\T^n)$. The Fouier transform is thus naturally defined $\mathcal{F}: L^2(\T^n) \to l^2(\Z^n)$ by

$$ \hat{f}(\kappa) = \langle f, E_\kappa \rangle = \int_{\T^n} f(x) \overline{E}_{\kappa}(x) dx = \int_{\T^n} f(x)e^{-2\pi i \kappa\cdot x}\;dx. $$

Parserval's identity states that $\Vert f\Vert_{L^2(\T^n)} = \Vert \hat{f}\Vert_{l^2(\Z^n)}$, i.e.,

$$ \begin{align} \int_{\T^n} |f(x)|^2 dx = \sum_{\kappa\in \Z^n} |\hat{f}(\kappa)|^2. \end{align} $$

The Fourier transform is an isometry and thus can be extended to a unitary operator on $L^2(\T^n)$. The theory of Hilbert spaces implies that the Fourier transform is invertible and the inverse is given by

$$ \begin{align}\label{eq:inverse_fourier} f(x) = \sum_{\kappa\in \Z^n} \hat {f}(\kappa) E_\kappa(x) = \sum_{\kappa\in \Z^n} \hat {f}(\kappa) e^{2\pi i \kappa \cdot x} \end{align}
$$

as functions in $L^2(\T^n)$. By Hausdorff-Young inequality, the Fourier transform can be extended to a bounded linear operator from $L^p(\T^n)$ to $l^q(\Z^n)$ for $1\leq p \leq 2$ and $\frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{q} = 1$. However, the Fourier transform is not invertible on $L^p(\T^n)$ for $p\neq 2$.

$$ \begin{align}\label{eq:hausdorff_young} \Vert \hat{f}\Vert_{l^q(\Z^n)} \leq \Vert f\Vert_{L^p(\T^n)}, \quad 1\leq p \leq 2, \frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{q} = 1. \end{align} $$

If $\widehat{f}\in \ell^1(\Z^n)$, then the Fourier series $f_N(x) = \sum_{\kappa \in \Z^n, |\kappa|\leq N} \widehat{f}(\kappa) e^{2\pi i \kappa \cdot x}$ converges uniformly to a continuous function $G\in C(\T^n)$. By the uniqueness of Fourier coefficient we obtain $F\equiv G$.
