Remark on a Bump function

2024-06-27

Basic properties

For $R>0$, let us define the smooth bump function $$ \Phi(x) = \frac{|x|^2}{1+|x|^2} \qquad \text{and}\qquad \Phi_R(x) = \Phi\left(\frac{x}{R}\right), \qquad x\in \R^d. $$

We have the computation \begin{align} & D^2\Phi(x) = \frac{2}{(1+|x|^2)^2}\left(\mathbb{I}_d - \frac{4x\otimes x}{1+|x|^2} \right) \\ & |D^2\Phi(x)| \leq \frac{2}{(1+|x|^2)}\left(d^{1/2} + \frac{4|x|^2}{1+|x|^2}\right) \leq 2(4+\sqrt{d}) \\ & |D^2\Phi_R(x)| \leq \frac{2(4+\sqrt{d})}{R^2} \\ & |\nabla_R\Phi(x) - \nabla \Phi_R(y)| \leq \frac{2(4+\sqrt{d})}{R^2}|x-y|. \end{align}

For $\gamma \in (0,1)$ we have

\begin{align} |\nabla \Phi_R(x) - \nabla \Phi_R(y)| & = |\nabla \Phi_R(x) - \nabla \Phi_R(y)|^{1-\gamma} \cdot |\nabla \Phi_R(x) - \nabla \Phi_R(y)|^{\gamma} \\ &\leq \left(\frac{2}{R}\right)^{1-\gamma}\left(\frac{2(4+\sqrt{d})}{R^2}\right)^{\gamma} |x-y|^{\gamma} \\ &= \frac{2(4+\sqrt{d})^\gamma}{R^{1+\gamma}}|x-y|^\gamma. \end{align}

We deduce that

\begin{equation} \Vert \Phi_R\Vert_{C^{1,\gamma}(\mathbb{R}^d)} \leq 1 + \frac{1}{R} + \frac{2(4+\sqrt{d})^\gamma}{R^{1+\gamma}} \leq \frac{C(d,\gamma)}{R^{1+\gamma}} \end{equation}

for $R>1$.

Touching function

Define for $x_0, \Theta, \delta, h$ the function $ \psi(x,t) = \Theta + \delta \Phi_R(x-x_0) + ht$ for $(x,t)\in \R^d\times [0,T]$. If $u$ is a viscosity subsolution to $\partial_t u = \mathcal{M}^+(u,x)$ and $\psi$ touches $u$ from above at $( x ^ *, t ^ * )$ then

$$ h = \partial_t \psi ( x ^ *, t ^ * ) \leq \mathcal{M} ^ + \big( \psi( \cdot , t ^ * ), x ^ * \big). $$

The goal is to show that, if $\delta>0$, there exists $x,\Theta, h, R$ such that the above equation implies a contradiction.

Lemma
Let $u:\R^d\times [0,T]\to \R$ be bounded from above and upper semicontinuous such that $$ \delta = \sup_{\R^d \times [0,T]} u - \sup_{\R^d} u(\cdot, 0) > 0. $$ Then there exists $\psi(x,t) = C_1 + C_2\Phi_R(x-x_0) + ht$ such that $\psi$ touches $u$ from above at some point $(x^*, t^*) \in \R^d\times (0,T]$.
Proof
Choose $(x_\delta, t_\delta) \in \R^d\times [0,T]$ such that $$ u(x_\delta, t_\delta) > \left( \sup_{\R^d\times [0,T]} u \right) - \frac{\delta}{4}. $$ We must have $t_\delta>0$, since otherwise, if $t_\delta= 0$ then $$ \left( \sup_{\R^d\times [0,T]} u \right) - \frac{\delta}{4} < u(x_\delta, 0) \leq \sup_{\R^d} u(\cdot, 0) = \left( \sup_{\R^d\times [0,T]} u \right) - \delta, $$ which is a contradiction. Note that $$ \Psi(x_\delta, t_\delta) = u(x_\delta, t_\delta) - h t_\delta \geq \sup_{\R^d} u(\cdot, 0) + \frac{3\delta}{4} - ht_\delta \geq \sup_{\R^d} u(\cdot, 0) + \frac{3\delta}{4} - hT $$ Therefore, if $hT \leq \frac{\delta}{4}$ then $$ \Psi(x_\delta, t_\delta) \geq \sup_{\R^d} u(\cdot, 0) + \frac{\delta}{2}. $$ On the other hand, for $|x-x_\delta|\geq \sqrt{3} R$ then $\Phi_R(x) \geq \frac{3}{4}$, thus $$ \Psi(x,t) \leq u(x,t) -\frac{3\delta}{4} - ht \leq \sup_{\R^d\times [0,T]} u - \frac{3\delta}{4} - \frac{\delta}{4} = \sup_{\R^d} u(\cdot, 0) $$ Since $\Psi$ is upper semicontinuous, it has maximum over the compact set $\overline{B } _ { \sqrt{3}R}(x_\delta)\times [0,T]$. Assume the maximum is achieved at $( x ^ *, t ^ * )$. Then $$ \Psi( x ^ *, t ^ * ) \geq \Psi(x_\delta, t_\delta) \geq \sup_{\R^d} u(\cdot, 0) + \frac{\delta}{2}. $$ This implies that $t^* > 0$ and the proof is complete.

However, we note that the maximum is not always postive (i.e., the constant $C$ in $C+\delta \Phi_R(x-x_\delta) + ht$), especially when

$$ \sup_{\R^d} u(\cdot, 0) < 0. $$

This is the reason why in most papers when dealing with comparison principle instead of compare $u(x,t) \leq u(x,0)$ they do $u_+(x,t) \leq u_+(x,0)$ instead, where

$$ u_+(x) = \max{u(x), 0} \geq 0. $$
