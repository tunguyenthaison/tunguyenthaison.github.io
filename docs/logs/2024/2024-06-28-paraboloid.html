Remark on a paraboloids

2024-06-28

Definitions

Paraboloids
A $\textbf{paraboloid}$ is a quadratic polynomial in $(x,t) = (x_1,x_2,\ldots, x_n,t) \in \R^d\times [0,T)$, which can be written as \begin{equation*} p(x,t) = c + (b, b_0)\cdot (x,t) + \frac{1}{2}x^T \cdot \mathbf{A}\cdot x + \frac{1}{2}a_0 t^2, \end{equation*} where $c\in \mathbb{R}$ and $(b,b_0) \in \mathbb{R}^d\times \R$ and $\mathbf{A} = D^2p(x)$ (is independent of $x$) is a symmetric matrix of size $d\times d$, and $a_0\in \R$. For $m>0$, we say that the paraboloid $p(\cdot)$ is of opening $m$ if \begin{align*} p(x,t) = c + (b, b_0)\cdot (x,t) \pm \frac{m}{2}(|x|^2+|t|^2). \end{align*} The paraboloid is convex (resp. concave) if the sign of the 2nd-order term is positive (resp. negative). We say that $u$ is touched from above at $(x_0,t_0)$ by a $\textbf{convex}$ paraboloid of opening $m$ if there exists $c\in \mathbb{R}$ and $(b,b_0)\in \mathbb{R}^d\times \R$ such that \begin{equation*} u(x,t) \leq u(x_0,t_0) + (b,b_0)\cdot (x-x_0, t-t_0) + \frac{m}{2} \big( |x-x_0|^2 + |t-t_0|^2 \big). \end{equation*} If this is the case, we also say that $u$ is $C^{1,1}$ by above at $(x_0,t_0)$. We also denote $$ u \in \frac{m}{2}-C^{1,1}(x_0,t_0). $$

The touching trick

For simplicity, if $u:\R^d\times [0,T]\to \R$ is differentiable at $(x_0,t_0)$ then we denote

$$ P_u(x,t) = u(x_0,t_0) + \partial_t u(x_0,t_0)\cdot (t-t_0) + \nabla u(x_0,t_0) \cdot (x-x_0) $$

as a affine functional (1st-order Taylor polynomal).

Let $u:\R^d\times [0,T] \to \R$ be bounded and upper-semicontinuous, and $v:\R^d\times [0,T] \to \R$ be bounded and lower-semicontinuous. Let $u^\varepsilon, v_\varepsilon$ be the sup-convolution and inf-convolution of $u,v$, respectively. Then, $u^\varepsilon$ is semiconvex, and $v_\varepsilon$ is semiconcave. In other words, at every point $(x_0,t_0)$ in the domain:

  • $u^\varepsilon$ can be touched from below by a paraboloid of opening $\frac{1}{2\varepsilon}$; i.e., $$ u^\varepsilon(x,t) \geq P_{u^\varepsilon}(x,t) - \frac{1}{2\varepsilon} \big(|x-x_0|^2 + |t-t_0|^2\big). $$
  • $v_\varepsilon$ can be touched from above by a paraboloid of opening $\frac{1}{2\varepsilon}$. $$ v^\varepsilon(x,t) \leq P_{v^\varepsilon}(x,t) + \frac{1}{2\varepsilon} \big(|x-x_0|^2 + |t-t_0|^2\big). $$

Assume $\phi(x,t)$ is a smooth function such that

$$ (u^\varepsilon - v_\varepsilon) - \phi \;\text{has a local maximum zero at}\;(x_0,t_0). $$

The tricks are:
1 $u^\varepsilon, v_\varepsilon$ are in $(2\varepsilon^{-1})-C^{1,1}(x_0,t_0)$ jointly in $(x,t)$.
2 $u^\varepsilon, v_\varepsilon$ are Hölder at $(x_0,t_0)$ with norm $C\varepsilon^{-1}$ and $C$ depends on $L^\infty$-bound $u,v$

Since $\phi$ is smooth, we have

$$ \phi(x,t) = P_\phi(x,t) + \circ(|x-x_0|^2+|t-t_0|^2) $$

and also

$$ P_{u^\varepsilon}(x_0,t_0) = P_{v_\varepsilon}(x_0,t_0) + P_\phi(x_0,t_0). $$

Let $$ \ell(x,t) = P_{u^\varepsilon}(x_0,t_0) - \Big( P_{v_\varepsilon}(x_0,t_0) + P_\phi(x_0,t_0) \Big) $$ be an affine function: $\ell(x,t) = c_\ell + (b_\ell, a_\ell)\cdot (x-x_0,t-t_0)$. Then $\ell(x_0,t_0) = 0$ implies $c_\ell = 0$.

Since $u^\varepsilon - (v_\varepsilon + \phi)$ has a local maximum at $(x_0,t_0)$, i.e.,

$$ \begin{align} P_{u^\varepsilon}(x,t) - \frac{1}{2\varepsilon} \big(|(x,t)-(x_0, t_0)|^2\big) &\leq u^\varepsilon(x,t) \\ &\leq v_\varepsilon(x,t) + \phi(x,t) \\ &\leq P_{v^\varepsilon}(x,t) + \frac{1}{2\varepsilon} \big(|(x,t)-(x_0, t_0)|^2\big) + \phi(x,t) \\ &\leq\Big( P_{v_\varepsilon}(x,t)+P_\phi(x,t)\Big) + \frac{1}{2\varepsilon} \big(|(x,t)-(x_0, t_0)|^2\big) + o(|(x,t)-(x_0, t_0)|^2). \end{align} $$

with equalities at $(x,t) = (x_0,t_0)$. We deduce that

$$ \begin{align} \ell(x,t) &\leq \frac{2}{\varepsilon}\Vert (x,t)-(x_0,t_0) \Vert^2 \end{align} $$ Recall that $$ \ell(x,t) = (b_\ell, a_\ell) \cdot \big( (x,t) - (x_0,t_0)\big). $$ If $a\cdot X \leq C|X|^2$ for all $X\in \R^n$, then the vector $a\in \R^n$ must be zero. Indeed, if $a\neq 0$, we can take $$ \begin{align} X = \frac{a}{2C} &\qquad \Longrightarrow\qquad a\cdot X = \frac{|a|^2}{2C} \leq C|X|^2 = C\cdot \frac{|a|^2}{4C^2} = \frac{|a|^2}{4C} \qquad \Longrightarrow\qquad 4C \leq 2C \end{align} $$ which is a contradiction. Thus we conclude that $\ell(x,t) \equiv 0$, i.e., $$ P(x,t) = P_{u^\varepsilon}(x,t) = P_{v_\varepsilon}(x,t) + P_\phi(x,t) $$ as an affine function. We then have $$ |u^\varepsilon(x,t) - P(x,t)| \leq \frac{2}{\varepsilon}\Vert (x,t) - (x_0,t_0) \Vert^2 $$ and thus $u^\varepsilon \in (2\varepsilon^{-1})-C^{1,1}(x_0,t_0)$-jointly. By a similar argument, we also have $v_\varepsilon \in (2\varepsilon^{-1})-C^{1,1}(x_0,t_0)$-jointly. Consequently, we have in a ball $B_r(x_0,t_0)$ then

$$ [ u^\varepsilon ]_{ C^{ 1,\gamma }(x_0,t_0) }, \leq (2\varepsilon^{-1}r^\gamma) \leq 2\varepsilon^{-1} $$

and

$$ [ v_\varepsilon ]_{ C^{ 1,\gamma }(x_0,t_0) } \leq (2\varepsilon^{-1}r^\gamma) \leq 2\varepsilon^{-1} $$ if we choose $r\leq 1$ and do not care about the vanishing as $r\to 0^+$. On the other hand, it is clear that

$$ \mathrm{Lip}(u^\varepsilon)\leq \frac{C}{\varepsilon}\Vert u\Vert_{L^\infty} \qquad \mathrm{Lip}(u_\varepsilon)\leq \frac{C}{\varepsilon}\Vert v\Vert_{L^\infty} $$
