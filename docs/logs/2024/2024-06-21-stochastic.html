Miscellaneous remarks

  • $n$-dimensional "white noise" $\xi$ is the time-derivative of $n$-dimensional Brownian motion (a.k.a. Wiener process): $\dot{\mathbf{W}}(x) = \xi(x)$.

  • Given a probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{M}, P)$, we call $\Omega$ a sample space, $\mathcal{M}$ the collection of all events, and $P(A)$ the probability of an event $A\in \mathcal{M}$.

  • Random variables: the probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{M}, P)$ is a mathematical construct only, i.e., it is not directly observable. We observe it via a map $X:\Omega \to \R^n$, where the values $X(\omega)$ we can observe (or measure). We therefore interested in the Borel $\sigma$-algebra of $\R^n$.

  • An $n$-dimensional random variable $X$ is a map $X:\Omega \to \R^n$ with $X^{-1}(\mathcal{B}) \subset \mathcal{M}$ (equivalently, $X$ is $\mathcal{M}$-measurable).

Some probabilistic tools

  • Borel-Cantelli Lemma Let $A_1,\ldots, A_n, \ldots$ be a sequence of events. Then the event $$ \bigcap_{n=1}^\infty \bigcup_{m=n}^\infty A_m = \{\omega \in \Omega: \omega\;\text{belongs to infinitely many}\;A_n\} = \{A_n\;\text{i.o.}\} $$ The Borel-Cantelli Lemma said that, if $\sum_{n=1}^\infty P(A_n) < \infty$ then $P(A_n\;\text{i.o.}) = 0$.
