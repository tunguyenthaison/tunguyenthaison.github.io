Some notes on Hele-Shaw equation

Some naive readings on the Hele-Shaw equation

Naive attempt

If the domain Ω{\Omega}Ω is given by Ω={(x,y)∈Tn×R:y<f(x,t)}\Omega = \{(x,y)\in \mathbb{T}^n\times \mathbb{R}: y < f(x,t)\}Ω={(x,y)∈Tn×R:y<f(x,t)}, then at a point x^=(x,f(x))∈∂Ω\hat{x} = (x,f(x))\in \partial \Omegax^=(x,f(x))∈∂Ω, the outward unit normal vector to ∂Ω\partial\Omega∂Ω is given by

$$ \mathbf{n}(\hat{x}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+|\nabla f(x)|^2}} \begin{pmatrix} - \nabla f(x) \ 1 \end{pmatrix}. $$

A boundary condition states that:

The normal velocity of the free surface is equal to the normal component of the fluid velocity on the free surface

This means, if Ωt={(x,y)∈Tn×R:y<f(x,t)}\Omega_t = \{(x,y)\in \mathbb{T}^n\times \mathbb{R}: y < f(x,t)\}Ωt​={(x,y)∈Tn×R:y<f(x,t)} is involving in ttt, then the free surface is given by

$$ \Sigma_t = \partial \Omega_t = \left \lbrace (x,f(x,t)): x\in \mathbb{T}^n\right \rbrace. $$

Then with this notation, what is the "normal velocity of the free surface"?

According to this post from physics.stackexchange titled Free surface in fluid dynamics, that

$$ \text{normal velocity of the free surface} = \mathbf{V}(t)\cdot \mathbf{n}(t) $$ where

  • V\mathbf{V}V is the free surface velocity (how to define this?)

  • n\mathbf{n}n is the unit normal to the surface.

Then the condition

The normal velocity of the free surface is equal to the normal component of the fluid velocity on the free surface

reads $$ \mathbf{V}\cdot \mathbf{n} = \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{n} \tag{1} $$ where u\mathbf{u}u is the fluid velocity, here u:Ω→Rn+1∋(x,y)\mathbf{u}:\Omega \to \mathbb{R}^{n+1} \ni (x,y)u:Ω→Rn+1∋(x,y) where yyy stands for the vertical variable. We write $$ y = f(x,t) $$ as the equation for the free surface Σt\Sigma_tΣt​. Each fluid particle on the free surface has two coordinates x,yx,yx,y and since the fluid particle moves with time, we see that yyy is a function of both xxx and ttt.

$$ \frac{dy}{dt} = f_t + \nabla f(x) \cdot{x}'(t). $$

The following are wrong!

If we look at a point on the surface of the form (x,y)=(x,f(x,t))(x,y) = (x,f(x,t))(x,y)=(x,f(x,t)) as times ttt changes. If we fix xxx then we should have $$ x'(t) = 0 $$ i.e., we only look at how the point move vertically as time ttt changes. Then the total velocity of the free surface is $$ \mathbf{V}(t) = \Big(x'(t), \frac{d}{dt}f(x,t)\Big) = (0, f_t). $$ Therefore from (1)(1)(1) we have

$$ \mathbf{V}\cdot \mathbf{n} = \frac{f_t}{\sqrt{1+|\nabla f|^2}} = \mathbf{u}\cdot \mathbf{n}. $$

This is probably wrong, as one needs to decompose the V\mathbf{V}V into a tangential component and a normal component, which then by some theorem, the tangential component V⋅t=0\mathbf{V}\cdot \mathbf{t} = 0V⋅t=0.
