## Naive attempt 
If the domain ${\Omega}$ is given by $\Omega = \{(x,y)\in \mathbb{T}^n\times \mathbb{R}: y < f(x,t)\}$, 
then at a point $\hat{x} = (x,f(x))\in \partial \Omega$, the **outward unit normal vector** to $\partial\Omega$ is given by


$$ 
    \mathbf{n}(\hat{x}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+|\nabla f(x)|^2}} 
    \begin{pmatrix}
        - \nabla f(x) \\
        1 
    \end{pmatrix}.
$$

A boundary condition states that:

> The normal velocity of the free surface is equal to the normal component of the fluid velocity on the free surface

This means, if $\Omega_t = \{(x,y)\in \mathbb{T}^n\times \mathbb{R}: y < f(x,t)\}$ is involving in $t$, then the free surface is given by 

$$
    \Sigma_t = \partial \Omega_t = \left\lbrace (x,f(x,t)): x\in \mathbb{T}^n\right \rbrace.
$$

Then with this notation, what is the *"normal velocity of the free surface"*?

According to this post from `physics.stackexchange` titled *[Free surface in fluid dynamics](https://physics.stackexchange.com/questions/407884/free-surface-in-fluid-dynamics)*, that 

$$
    \text{normal velocity of the free surface} = \mathbf{V}(t)\cdot \mathbf{n}(t)
$$
where 

* $\mathbf{V}$ is the free surface velocity (how to define this?)

* $\mathbf{n}$ is the unit normal to the surface. 

Then the condition 

> The normal velocity of the free surface is equal to the normal component of the fluid velocity on the free surface

reads
$$ 
    \mathbf{V}\cdot \mathbf{n} = \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{n}  \tag{1}
$$
where $\mathbf{u}$ is the fluid velocity, here $\mathbf{u}:\Omega \to \mathbb{R}^{n+1} \ni (x,y)$ where $y$ stands for the vertical variable. We write 
$$
    y = f(x,t) 
$$
as the equation for the free surface $\Sigma_t$. Each fluid particle on the free surface has two coordinates $x,y$ and since the fluid particle moves with time, we see that $y$ is a function of both $x$ and $t$. 

$$
    \frac{dy}{dt}  = f_t + \nabla f(x) \cdot{x}'(t). 
$$

> The following are wrong!
> 
> ---
> 
> If we look at a point on the surface of the form $(x,y) = (x,f(x,t))$ as times $t$ changes. If we fix $x$ then we should have 
> $$
>     x'(t) = 0 
> $$
> i.e., we only look at how the point move vertically as time $t$ changes. Then the total velocity of the free surface is
> $$ 
>     \mathbf{V}(t) = \Big(x'(t), \frac{d}{dt}f(x,t)\Big) = (0, f_t).
> $$
> Therefore from $(1)$ we have
> 
> $$
>     \mathbf{V}\cdot \mathbf{n} = \frac{f_t}{\sqrt{1+|\nabla f|^2}} = \mathbf{u}\cdot \mathbf{n}.
> $$
> 
> This is probably wrong, as one needs to decompose the $\mathbf{V}$ into a tangential component and a normal component, which then by some theorem, the tangential component $\mathbf{V}\cdot \mathbf{t} = 0$.
